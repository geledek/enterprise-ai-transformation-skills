# Mongolian Teachers — NotebookLM over Advanced Prompting, Agent Skills, and Manus

**Year:** 2026
**Group:** School teachers in Mongolia (training delivered in Mongolian)
**Choice set:** More advanced prompting on existing chatbots · agent skills · Manus (new power tool) · Google NotebookLM (new tool)
**Outcome:** NotebookLM selected — Introduce mode, with advanced prompting deferred to a later module

## Story

A trainer with a free choice of tool had one session with school teachers who were already familiar with ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude. The session opened with the CRAFT prompting framework as a shared baseline. The live decision was what to build the rest of the training on: deeper prompting technique, agent skills on top of the chatbots they knew, or a new power tool (Manus was the candidate).

The trainer chose Google NotebookLM instead. The deciding factors were not capability ceilings — Manus and agent-skill setups arguably have higher ceilings — but constraints and delta: NotebookLM required no additional setup to produce video overviews and presentation decks, it is free to use, and it lives inside the Google Suite the teachers' schools already subscribed to.

## Diagnostic

**Role 1 — Audience Profiler.** Baseline was already solid: teachers used general chatbots. The artifact inventory was the tell — teachers produce lesson materials, presentations, and study aids weekly. "A better chatbot answer" improves what they already make; video and slide generation from their own source material is a new artifact class.

**Role 2 — Constraint Mapper.** The eliminators did most of the work. Manus: new account, unfamiliar ecosystem, cost exposure — eliminated on setup friction and cost before capability was ever compared. Agent skills: require per-tool setup and someone to own the skill library after the trainer leaves — eliminated on setup friction and day-2 maintenance for a one-session engagement. NotebookLM: free, zero setup, inside the existing Google subscription — passed every filter.

**Role 3 — Intervention-Mode Selector.** Baseline fluency made Deepen a weak increment (CRAFT already covered the high-value share of prompting technique in the opening). Extend failed the maintainer condition. Introduce was right because a surviving candidate unlocked new artifact classes. Flip condition: with a multi-week program and a named internal maintainer, Extend (a shared skill/template library) becomes the better play.

**Role 4 — Capability-Delta Assessor.** NotebookLM's delta: upload existing teaching materials, get video overviews, audio, and structured decks — artifact classes the chatbots don't produce — at minutes-to-first-output friction. Advanced prompting's delta: better versions of already-possible artifacts. New-class beat better-same.

**Role 5 — First-Win & Day-2 Realist.** First win fits the window: each teacher uploads their own lesson material and generates an overview in one sitting. Day-2 is favorable — the tool lives where the teachers already work (Google), so no new habit loop or login to sustain. Blast radius: outputs reach students, so teacher review before classroom use is the standing rule.

## Verdict

**Adopt-now (Introduce mode).** First-win exercise: generate a video overview from your own lesson document. Growth path: advanced prompting as module 2, applied *inside* NotebookLM and the chatbots they already use. Weakest link: language — output quality in Mongolian should be spot-checked against classroom standards before teachers rely on generated video/audio, which is the argument for the teacher-review rule.

## The pattern this case encodes

Constraints eliminated the highest-ceiling candidates before capability comparison ever happened, and the winner was the tool with the largest *new-artifact* delta at the lowest friction — not the most powerful tool. For one-session trainings with an artifact-producing audience, zero-setup + existing-ecosystem + new-artifact-class beats ceiling every time.
