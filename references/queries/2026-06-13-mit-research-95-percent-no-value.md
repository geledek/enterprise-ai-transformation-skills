## Question
Why did MIT research say 95% of organisations did not get much value despite huge investment and consumption?

## Answer

The finding comes from **MIT NANDA's 2025 GenAI Divide Study**, which analyzed enterprise GenAI deployment outcomes across industries. The key claim is:

> **95% of enterprise GenAI pilots return zero measurable P&L impact.** The remaining 5% capture nearly all the value.

(source: [[95-5-genai-divide]])

### Why do 95% fail to capture value?

The MIT NANDA research found that the 5% who succeed **don't have better models** — they make different *structural choices* about what to build, how to scope it, and what to measure. The failures cluster in three patterns:

1. **Wrong scope + wrong execution:** Organisations attempt broad-scope, complex-execution deployments simultaneously — end-to-end orchestration of complex business processes. The Scope × Execution Grid shows this quadrant is where most failures live. (source: [[95-5-genai-divide]])

2. **Buy vs. Build mismatch:** Firms that buy AI capability rather than build it internally are **2× more likely to capture value**. Many organisations invest heavily in building custom models when proven bought solutions would outperform. (source: [[95-5-genai-divide]])

3. **Stage 1–2 trap:** MIT CISR's parallel research on 721 firms (2022 survey) shows that **62% of organisations are stuck in Stages 1–2** of AI maturity (experimenting or piloting), where financial performance is *below* industry average (−12.6 pp and −3.5 pp respectively). Only Stage 3+ firms outperform. Most investment never crosses the Stage 2 → Stage 3 inflection point because no single pilot pays for the shared platform investment required. (source: [[mit-cisr-4-stages]])

### The Scope × Execution Grid (what the 5% do differently)

| | Narrow Scope | Broad Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple Execution** | Fast wins ✓ | Partial pilots |
| **Complex Execution** | Early pilots | Fails ✗ |

The 5% start upper-left (narrow + simple) and expand outward — either broader scope *or* more complex execution, never both at once. Successful fast wins: voice AI for call summarization, document automation for contracts, code generation — all landing in 6–12 months.

### In plain terms

Organisations are investing in AI but deploying it at the wrong scope and complexity, measuring the wrong outcomes (tokens used rather than P&L impact), and building what should be bought. The investment is real; the problem is architectural and strategic, not financial.

(source: [[95-5-genai-divide]], [[mit-cisr-4-stages]])
