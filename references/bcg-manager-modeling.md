# BCG Manager Role-Modeling in AI Adoption

## What this reference contains

BCG Build for the Future × AI 2026 finding on the gap between managers who believe AI role-modeling matters and managers who actually do it. The gap is the largest single lever for the People 70%.

## Key claims (with citations)

- **88% of managers believe role-modeling AI use is important.** Source: BCG BFFxAI 2026.
- **Only 25% of managers actually do it visibly.** Source: BCG BFFxAI 2026.
- **The 88/25 gap is the most actionable single lever in the People layer.** The majority of managers believe they should model AI use — they just don't. The barrier is behavioral, not attitudinal.
- **Modeling must be visible.** Using AI privately in preparation doesn't count. The behavior that moves employee adoption is seeing the manager use AI in meetings, reviews, and shared work artifacts.
- **Manager behavior is the primary driver of employee AI adoption rates.** Source: BCG BFFxAI 2026.

## The role-modeling prescription

Specific behaviors that qualify as visible role-modeling:
- Using AI outputs in team meetings (sharing and discussing AI-generated analysis)
- Running AI prompts in front of direct reports
- Publicly crediting AI assistance in work products
- Assigning AI-augmented tasks to team members and reviewing the AI output together

## When to consult

- For skill `readiness-conversation`: the 88/25 gap is the opening diagnostic for manager readiness — ask "what percentage of your managers are visibly using AI in team settings?" A gap below 25% is a named risk.
- For skill `maturity-assessment`: Accenture Achievers have 83% CEO + senior sponsorship; the BCG manager finding extends this down the org — role-modeling cascades from C-suite to managers to employees.
